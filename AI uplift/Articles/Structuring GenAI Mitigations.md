# Article: Structuring GenAI Mitigations: A Playbook for Delivery Leaders

## The Mitigation Imperative
Identifying the risks associated with Generative AI is only the first step. Orchestrating the right mitigations to neutralise those risks is the true test of enterprise delivery. 

Generative AI introduces highly specific vulnerabilities that traditional software projects rarely encounter. As a result, the technology requires a highly structured approach to safety. The core challenge for Delivery Leaders is orchestrating a careful balance. Successful deployment depends on weaving together rigorous technical design choices with robust, non-technical governance guardrails.

## The Eight Categories of GenAI Risk
Before an organisation can build effective mitigations, it must first thoroughly map the risk landscape. According to leading industry frameworks, there are eight primary categories of Generative AI risk that programme orchestrators must monitor.

These categories include impaired fairness from biased training data, intellectual property infringement, and data privacy breaches. Furthermore, one must account for malicious use, overarching security threats, and issues surrounding the performance and explainability of the model. Finally, there are strategic risks regarding the business direction and third-party risks associated with relying on external AI vendors.

## Technical Mitigations: Coding for Safety
Technical mitigations are the hard controls engineered directly into the software architecture and the AI model itself. These are primarily owned by the engineering and architecture teams, but the Delivery Leader must track them rigorously.

Practical examples of technical mitigations include deploying strict role-based access controls. This ensures that the AI cannot regurgitate sensitive financial data to an employee who lacks the correct clearance. Other technical measures include programming the model to proactively ask clarifying questions when faced with ambiguous prompts, which greatly reduces the chance of hallucinations. Delivery teams must also focus on restricting the model's access away from highly sensitive proprietary databases.

## Non-Technical Mitigations: Governing the Human Element
Technology alone cannot guarantee safety. Non-technical mitigations are just as critical. These rely on intelligent process design, human oversight, and comprehensive user training. 

The most prominent non-technical mitigation is enforcing a "human in the loop" policy. This means an AI system is never allowed to execute a critical decision without a qualified human validating the output first. Other vital non-technical controls include designing the user interface to provide clear source citations alongside any AI-generated answers. This allows the user to quickly verify the accuracy of the information. Above all, organisations must distribute strict acceptable use guidelines and conduct thorough training for all employees who interact with the tool.

## Conclusion
A mature Delivery Leader understands that these risk mitigations cannot exist in isolation. Relying solely on engineers to build technical firewalls is not enough. Likewise, relying entirely on user training leaves the organisation vulnerable to systemic errors. One must weave both technical controls and robust governance tightly together into a single, cohesive delivery plan. By adopting this blended approach, organisations can scale their AI portfolios at pace whilst keeping their data, their customers, and their reputation completely secure.

# Article: Delivering Enterprise GenAI: Balancing Speed with Judicious Governance

## The GenAI Balancing Act
Generative AI offers transformative capabilities for productivity and innovation. However, many organisations feel unprepared to initiate it in a responsible way. 

The primary challenge for Delivery Leaders is navigating the tension between moving quickly and implementing judicious risk management. An overly cautious approach risks missing the true potential of the technology. Conversely, moving without guardrails can lead to missteps that erode stakeholder confidence. The solution lies in building fit for purpose risk management approaches.

## Inbound Threats vs Adoption Risks
Organisations face AI risks on two fronts. Firstly, they face inbound threats such as malicious deepfakes and generative AI enabled malware, even if they have not deployed their own internal AI models. 

Secondly, when an organisation does adopt AI, it must oversee the mitigation of adoption risks. These exposures fall into several key categories. Common risks include impaired fairness from biased data, intellectual property infringement, data privacy breaches, and third party risks. Organisations must ensure that cross-functional controls are in place to prevent sensitive information from being exposed to public models.

## Adapting, Not Inventing, Governance Structures
A frequent mistake in AI project delivery is attempting to build entirely new governance committees from scratch. This approach often adds friction to decision making and causes confusion over accountability. 

Great AI delivery relies on organisations adapting existing governance structures to respond to the demand for AI approvals. One should establish a cross-functional, responsible AI steering group. This group should operate with at least a monthly cadence and contain members from the business, technology, legal, and compliance domains. This body serves to validate risk assessments and set principles on the acceptable use of AI, such as defining when human oversight is strictly required.

## The Four Critical Roles for Responsible AI
Successful implementation of Generative AI requires embedding a culture of responsibility across the delivery team. There are four critical roles required to push AI use cases forward safely.

**1. Product Managers / Use-Case Owners**
These individuals steer the direction of AI deployment. They are accountable for identifying new use cases, managing relevant risks, and building trust in the proposition that the business value can be achieved safely.

**2. Engineers**
Engineers are the technical experts who understand the mechanics of the models. They are accountable for guiding the technical feasibility of mitigations and coding the solutions required to limit risk.

**3. Governors**
The governance team helps establish the core risk frameworks and guardrails to guide the work of designers and engineers. They cover critical areas like data privacy, cybersecurity, and regulatory compliance.

**4. Users**
End users represent the final and most important line of defence. They need to be trained on the dynamics of the technology. Users play a critical role in helping identify risks and reporting problematic outputs during their interactions with the model.

## Conclusion
AI program orchestrators excel at aligning these four critical roles within a robust, adapted governance framework. By establishing clear accountability and streamlined oversight, organisations can ensure they capture the value of Generative AI at pace and with complete safety.

## Strength, Weakness & Frustration

**Strength - making complex delivery navigable**
- Turn multi-party, pressured situations into clear choices with consequences
- Shell Pilot: split launch from data load, two problems, one path
- Costa: diagnosed three root causes, fixed the handoffs, velocity tripled without adding headcount
- Shell POS: decoupled delivery paths instead of absorbing the vendor delay
- *Where I add most value is making the situation navigable, for the team, for stakeholders and for the business*

**Weakness - difficulty saying no in the moment**
- In collaborative, flat environments, instinct is to find a way to accommodate urgent stakeholder requests mid-sprint rather than park them
- In the short term that feels helpful; over time it erodes team focus and makes velocity unpredictable
- Working on it: Costa taught me that absorbing informal requests is the root cause, not a solution; now use DoR/DoD as the gate, makes it process not personal
- *I've learned that protecting the team's focus is more helpful than accommodating every request*

**What frustrates you - late risk surfacing**
- Known risks that don't travel until options have narrowed
- Decisions deferred past the point where the team can still manoeuvre
- It's a process frustration, not a people one, the information was usually there
- Response: build cadences that surface dependencies early and make it safe to flag risk
- Costa DoR/DoD and Shell Steering Committee cadences are both examples of building that structure
- *Late escalation is almost always avoidable, my instinct is to build the structure that prevents it*

**8. Multi-party go-live under pressure** *(Reserve)*

*Likely question: You're leading a high-profile launch. The stakeholder wants an aggressive go-live. A key agency is behind. Internal product has open gaps. Talk me through how you handle it.*

> **Frame:**
> - Three parties misaligned simultaneously: stakeholder, agency and internal product
> - First move: reframe the question, not "are we on track?" but "what is the minimum viable go-live and what are the trade-offs?"
> - Goal is controlled delivery with no surprises, not delivering everything
>
> **Actions:**
> - Rapid triage same day: product gaps (Day 1 critical vs deferrable); agency blockers (challenge vague answers with specifics); critical path (must-have vs nice-to-have)
> - Redefine go-live scope into three layers: Day 1 critical, controlled degradation (known limitations, documented and accepted), post-go-live backlog (committed not abandoned)
> - Stakeholder conversation with options not problems: keep date and reduce scope; partial rollout; delay with quantified commercial risk
> - Agency: move from passive tracking to active control; lock backlog to Day 1 critical path; daily cadence; escalate if commitments slip
> - Internal product: escalate with business case not delivery risk; push for workaround or partial release; if none available, document as known limitation
>
> **Anchor:**
> - Shell multi-market go-live; third-party POS vendor release cycle fell out of alignment
> - Worked with Technical SMEs to decouple impacted changes and route delivery through an adjacent component
> - Market released on time; dependency map restructured to protect subsequent markets
>
> **Punchline:** Success here is not perfect delivery, it is controlled delivery with no surprises. My job is to ensure we go live in a way that protects customer experience and long-term stakeholder trust.

### The in-store vs digital gap (know this cold)

**Core gap: serendipity (in-store) vs search (online)**

- **Discovery vs intent** — browse in-store, search online; nobody opens the site to be surprised
- **Staff curation vs algorithm** — handwritten cards carry personal trust; "customers also bought" is Amazon's game
- **Dwell time** — café keeps people in-store; online sessions are short, cart abandonment high
- **Events and community** — signings, book groups build loyalty that a points balance doesn't capture
- **Loyalty data blind spot** — Plus card captures purchases; in-store behavioural signals (frequency, conversations, events) invisible to the platform

**Delivery framing for Martin:**
- Platform migration risk isn't just technical — it's losing the editorial, curated brand feel in transition
- Platform must serve the discovery experience, not just deliver transactions

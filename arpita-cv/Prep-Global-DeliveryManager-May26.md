## Info - Global's Digital Products

- **Global Player** - streaming and on-demand app (iOS/Android); live radio, catch-up, podcasts, exclusive content; covers all Global stations (Heart, Capital, Capital XTRA, Smooth, Classic FM, LBC, Radio X, Gold)
- **DAX** - Global's digital audio advertising platform; one of Europe's largest; connects advertisers to listeners across Global's owned stations and third-party audio inventory
- **Smart speaker / voice** - Global Player available on Alexa and Google Home; live radio and on-demand; growing surface alongside the app
- **Transformation context** - Global is shifting from broadcast radio to digital audio at scale; the Transformation team owns the technology and product roadmaps behind this shift; digital monetisation (DAX) and audience growth (Global Player) are the two main commercial levers
  - **Broadcast radio** = one-way transmission; same signal to everyone; audience data is survey-based (RAJAR); no individual listener identity; no on-demand; no personalisation
  - **Digital audio** = app or smart speaker; individual login; on-demand listening; personalisation; precise behavioural data (what you played, skipped, searched); targetable advertising
  - **At scale** = Global already has ~25M weekly listeners on broadcast; the challenge is converting that existing audience to digital without losing them, while also attracting new listeners who never used FM
  - In practice this means: building and improving Global Player, integrating with smart speakers, launching on-demand and podcast content, and making DAX the platform advertisers use to reach that digital audience
  - The shift is commercially driven: a logged-in digital listener is worth significantly more to an advertiser than an anonymous FM listener - same person, more data, higher CPM

### Value chain

**Content providers (labels, talent, podcasters)** → **Brand/sports partners (McLaren etc. - content + B2B fees)** → **Global platform (broadcast + Global Player)** → **Listeners (audience at scale)** → **Advertisers pay via DAX** → **Revenue**

**Content (upstream)**
- Music licensed from record labels (Universal, Sony, Warner) via PPL/PRS
- On-air talent and programming produced in-house per station (Heart breakfast, Capital, LBC presenters, Classic FM)
- Podcasts: mix of in-house produced and third-party hosted on Global Player

**Distribution**
- **Broadcast** - FM/DAB radio transmitters; reaches ~25M listeners per week (RAJAR); audience measured by survey, broad demographic data only
- **Digital (Global Player)** - app and smart speakers; individual listener data, on-demand behaviour, precise targeting capability; this is what makes digital inventory more valuable to advertisers than broadcast

**Customers**
- **Advertisers** - the primary paying customer; buy airtime on stations and/or digital audio inventory via DAX; revenue is CPM-based (cost per thousand listeners)
- **Listeners** - the product sold to advertisers, not primarily the payer; audience scale and data quality is what Global sells
- **Global Player Premium subscribers** - small paying segment; ad-free listening and exclusive content; not the primary revenue model
- **Third-party publishers (DAX)** - DAX aggregates audio inventory beyond Global's own stations; independent podcasters and audio publishers plug into DAX to monetise their content; Global takes a platform cut

**Brand and sports partnerships - where McLaren fits**

_These sit in a hybrid position in the value chain - not standard content licensors, not standard advertisers. They are simultaneously content providers, B2B revenue generators and audience acquisition channels._

- **What they are:** official partnership deals where a brand (McLaren, sports clubs, entertainment properties) becomes an audio content partner of Global Player; e.g. Global Player as McLaren's official audio partner for F1 racing
- **Content flow:** the brand provides or co-produces premium audio content (race commentary, behind-the-scenes podcasts, driver interviews, exclusive access); Global distributes it on Global Player
- **B2B revenue:** the brand pays Global for the official partner designation, distribution reach and often production services - this is a direct fee, separate from the listener → advertiser model
- **Advertising layer:** the brand frequently also buys advertising inventory around their own content via DAX - they appear on both sides of the transaction (content partner and advertiser)
- **Audience acquisition:** McLaren promotes Global Player to their own fanbase (F1 fans); Global gets audience growth without paying for it; this is the strategic value beyond the fee
- **Why this matters commercially:** F1 fans are a high-income, brand-loyal, male-skewing demographic - exactly the kind of first-party audience data that makes DAX inventory more valuable to luxury and automotive advertisers
- **Other similar partnerships:** sports clubs, Premier League, entertainment studios, film/TV tie-ins, music artists with exclusive content - all follow the same model
- **Where it sits in the value chain:** between content providers and the platform; they provide content AND pay fees AND drive audience, making them the most commercially efficient input in the chain

**DAX - how it works**

- **What it is:** programmatic and direct digital audio advertising marketplace; launched 2014; one of Europe's first
- **How ads are bought:** advertisers bid in real-time (RTB) for audio ad slots across Global's own stations and third-party audio publishers plugged into DAX
- **Targeting levers:** demographic (age, gender), geographic (postcode/region), contextual (station genre, mood, time of day), behavioural (listening history from logged-in Global Player users)
- **First-party data advantage:** logged-in Global Player users provide consent-based first-party data; increasingly valuable as third-party cookies disappear; this is what DAX has that broadcast cannot offer
- **Dynamic ad insertion:** ads inserted into on-demand and podcast content at point of streaming, not baked in; allows targeting and frequency capping per listener
- **Third-party inventory:** DAX aggregates independent podcasters and audio publishers; Global takes a platform cut; this makes DAX a marketplace, not just Global's own ad sales tool
- **Why it matters for the role:** if the squad is DAX-facing, the DM needs to understand the advertiser-side product (targeting, reporting, campaign management tools) not just the listener-side product

#### Technical Literacy - Software Development and AI/ML Patterns

_JD requires strong working knowledge of software development practices, methodologies and release lifecycles, with awareness of AI/ML development patterns. Likely to come up as how comfortable are you working closely with engineers? or what's your awareness of AI/ML development?_

**SDLC - she lives and breathes this; reference as needed**

Requirements · Analysis · Design · Build · Test · Deploy · Maintain · Agile (Scrum / Kanban / SAFe) · Waterfall · Hybrid · Sprint planning · Backlog grooming · Retrospectives · DoR / DoD · CI/CD · Release management · Change control · Go/No-Go · UAT · Regression · Smoke testing · Version control (Git) · Feature flags · Canary releases · Blue-green deployment · Technical debt · APIs · Microservices

**Canary releases:**
- New version deployed to a small subset of users or traffic first (1-5%)
- Real production traffic, limited blast radius
- Monitor error rates, latency, conversion before rolling out to everyone
- If metrics degrade: halt rollout or auto-rollback
- Reduces Change Failure Rate: catches production issues early with minimal customer impact
- Pre-Prod catches functional defects; Canary validates behaviour under real production conditions

**Blue-Green deployments:**
- Two identical production environments run side by side: Blue (current live), Green (new version)
- Deploy new code to Green, run smoke tests, then switch traffic via load balancer
- Blue kept as immediate rollback option if Green fails
- No gradual rollout; a controlled switch with near-instant rollback capability
- Significantly reduces MTTR; rollback measured in seconds not hours
- Shell parallel: market-based feature configuration worked well alongside this approach

**AI/ML development patterns - know these at DM level**

- **Model training** - process of feeding labelled data into an algorithm to produce a model; compute-intensive; can take hours or days; has distinct phases: data preparation → training → evaluation → hyperparameter tuning; delivery implication: training runs are not instant and can fail - budget time for iteration
- **Model versioning** - like software version control but for models; a model retrained on new data is a new version; delivery implication: need to track which model version is deployed in which environment; rollback is possible but more complex than code rollback
- **Deployment patterns** - how a trained model goes from training environment to production; model serving infrastructure (APIs, latency SLAs); monitoring for performance degradation post-deployment
- **Model drift** - a deployed model's predictions degrade over time as real-world data shifts away from training data; requires ongoing monitoring and periodic retraining; delivery implication: done for an AI feature is not the same as traditional software - it needs a monitoring and retraining lifecycle built in
- **A/B testing / hypothesis-driven development** - the standard release mechanism for AI features; deploy a new model version to a subset of users, measure outcomes against a control group, decide whether to promote; this IS the rapid prototyping and hypothesis-driven development the JD references explicitly
- **Data pipelines as the critical path** - AI features depend on data flowing from source systems into the model; in many squads the blocker is a data dependency, not an engineering one; a DM needs to surface data pipeline readiness as early as code readiness
- **LLMs (Large Language Models)** - GPT, Claude and similar; for Global likely relevant to content recommendation, search, ad targeting, personalisation; prompt engineering is a development activity with its own iteration cycle; output quality is probabilistic, not deterministic - guardrails and human review are part of the delivery scope
- **RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation)** - common enterprise LLM pattern; the model is given relevant retrieved context (from a company database or knowledge store) before generating a response; grounds LLM output in company-specific data rather than general training; common in internal tools and search features
- **Model evaluation metrics** - unlike software (pass/fail), AI quality is probabilistic; accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score; a DM needs to understand that testing complete means something different for an AI feature and that acceptance criteria need to be framed around metric thresholds, not binary outcomes

### Modern Delivery Processes

_JD explicitly calls out: rapid prototyping, hypothesis-driven development, AI-assisted workflows. Brief keywords below for quick recall._

**Rapid prototyping - two forms:**

- UX prototype: Figma or InVision mockup; tests design and user flow before any engineering; no real code; throwaway
- Technical PoC: rough working code; tests feasibility of an approach before committing to full build; also throwaway
- Both validate assumptions cheaply before spending the full cost of building
- Real story: Shell pilot data ingestion; ran Pilot on a data subset to prove the approach before full rollout; same principle

**Hypothesis-driven development:**

- Start with a belief: "we think X will improve Y"
- Build the smallest version to test it
- Measure the result against a control
- Iterate, pivot or proceed based on evidence
- Connected to A/B testing and canary releases in practice
- Real story: Estee Lauder pilot brands; 5 brands first, proved the conversion lift, then scaled to all 17

### Way of Working Q&A

**Q: Walk me through how you work with a PO and Tech Lead day to day**

> - PO, Tech Lead, DM: three anchors of successful delivery
> - PO: ensure available to the team, supported, not a bottleneck
> - Tech Lead: clear on upcoming milestones and product requirements; focused on subject matter

**Q: What does your delivery style look like in practice?**

> - Collaborative; team clear on what they are delivering and why
> - Celebrate wins; work through issues together; learn from failures openly
> - Protect from scope creep
> - Document: risks, mitigations, decision paths, options considered; helps when looking back at why a direction was chosen
> - Look ahead: anticipate and resolve blockers before they land

**Q: How do you run a sprint?**

> - Backlog refinement: stories shaped and ready before planning
> - Sprint planning: prioritise, story point, commit
> - Kick-off: team clear on sprint goals from day one
> - Daily stand-up: blockers resolved, not just surfaced

> - Weekly: stakeholder status updates, dependency tracking, escalations where needed

### Handling "Their In-House Context" Questions

_Questions about the interviewer's specific internal problem - e.g. "we missed NFRs and now payment transactions are very slow at go-live, what do you do?" Do not attempt to solve their specific technical problem. You will never know their landscape. The test is how you think under pressure with incomplete information._

**What NOT to do:**
- Bluff a specific technical answer; you will get details wrong and a technical interviewer will know immediately
- Freeze or deflect entirely
- Pretend to know their stack

**The framework:**

> - Name the stakes: payment latency = abandoned transactions, lost revenue, SLA breaches, reputational risk
> - Signal the knowledge boundary: "I won't speculate on the technical fix, but from a delivery perspective..."
> - Triage structurally: what you would want to understand first
>   - Where in the transaction flow is the latency?
>   - What does "very long" mean vs baseline? Is it measured?
>   - Is the go-live deadline hard or negotiable?
>   - Can scope be throttled at launch?
> - Separate the problems: go-live decision and performance fix are two different tracks
> - Options with consequences: delay to fix, or go live with monitoring thresholds; business decides with clear eyes
>   - If going live: canary release; 1-5% of users first; monitor real production latency; roll back or proceed; controls exposure while fix runs in parallel
> - Anchor: Shell pilot data ingestion; proceed with what works, fix in parallel, scope and accept the risk

**The meta-principle:**
> A DM's value is not the technical answer; it is the ability to structure an ambiguous high-pressure problem into tractable tracks without pretending to know things you do not know

### Story Points, Velocity and QA - Practical Reality

**Should story points include dev and test effort?**

> - Theoretically: yes; story points represent full effort to reach Definition of Done including QA
> - In practice at scale: dev effort and QA effort are often tracked separately due to resource constraints
> - With a 30:5 dev/QA ratio, sprint-complete testing is structurally impossible without heavy automation
> - Story points estimate development complexity; QA effort tracked as separate tasks within the story

**Jira velocity and stories spanning sprints:**

> - Jira counts a story toward velocity in the sprint it is marked Done, not when dev completes
> - Story dev-complete in Sprint 1, QA-complete in Sprint 2: counts fully toward Sprint 2 velocity
> - Sprint 1 is understated; Sprint 2 is inflated; velocity becomes lumpy and unreliable for planning

**What to do in non-FLOW metric organisations:**

> - Use 3-sprint rolling average for planning; never plan against a single sprint velocity number
> - Add a "Dev Complete" custom status in Jira between In Progress and Done; makes QA lag visible without changing velocity calculation
> - Plan carry-over explicitly in sprint planning: separate buckets for QA on carry-over stories and dev on new stories
> - Track QA throughput separately; dev and QA velocity are different numbers; conflating them masks the bottleneck
> - Report carry-over rate as a visibility metric; consistently high carry-over is a structured conversation about QA capacity, not a performance failure
> - Punchline: plan honestly around the constraint and make the QA lag visible rather than hiding it inside a velocity number no one trusts

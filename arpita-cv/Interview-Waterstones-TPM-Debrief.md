# Waterstones TPM — Interview Debrief

**Date:** 29 April 2026, 4:00pm-4:45pm
**Interviewer:** Martin Hearn, Head of Digital
**Format:** Teams call
**Duration:** 45 minutes

---

## Interviewer Profile — Martin Hearn

Derived from the questions asked and what Martin revealed about the role.

**What he cares about most**

- **Requirements clarity and prioritisation** — the core job is taking requirements from business functions, clarifying them and giving them to developers in priority order. This is an embedded BA-TPM role, not a programme governance role.
- **Technical proximity** — asked about FE tools, JavaScript, SQL Server, Jira and Confluence. He wants someone who can hold a credible conversation with developers, not just manage process above them.
- **Practical GenAI usage** — asked explicitly. Signals he is aware of AI tooling and expects the TPM to be actively using it, not just aware of it.
- **Culture fit** — asked whether Arpita is a reader and what her favourite ecommerce site is. Consumer brand; the team will care about genuine affinity with the product and the customer.
- **Hands-on dev team working** — probed specifically for frontend experience, not just programme-level dev team management. Small team (15 developers), expects the TPM to be close to the work.

**What the role actually is**

- 15 developers, in-house team
- Business functions give requirements directly to the TPM
- TPM clarifies, prioritises and hands to developers
- Closer to a delivery-focused BA or embedded Product Owner than a programme governance TPM
- Most relevant CV story: Costa Coffee intake process redesign (STAR 7 in the guide) — not used in the interview

**Style**

- Conversational and direct, flat hierarchy feel, similar to Costa
- Questions were exploratory and practical, not formal competency probes
- Mix of culture fit, technical literacy and delivery experience — broad scan rather than deep STAR drilling

## Questions, Answers Given, and Feedback

---

### 1. Interviewer explained the programme

Martin opened by describing the role and team context before asking questions.

**What this signals:** He is transparent and collaborative. He was scoping the candidate against a specific operational reality, not a generic TPM job spec. The role shape (15 developers, business functions as requirements source, no PO) was given here — prep should have been built around this model.

---

### 2. Tell me about yourself

**Answer given:** Overall background in ecommerce, loyalty, web and mobile applications. Running multiple workstreams with Agile teams, in-house and external vendors.

**Assessment:** Undersold. The prepped version had Shell GO+ numbers (10M+ customers, four EMEA markets, tripled velocity at Costa) and a specific punchline about why Waterstones is the right fit. What was said is a domain list, not a story. This is the one answer that must be delivered as written, not improvised. The numbers and named examples are what validate seniority — without them the intro is interchangeable with any candidate.

**Stronger version:** Use the prepped intro verbatim — background sentence, Shell GO+ as the most relevant anchor with scale, Costa as the Agile and team transformation evidence, close with why Waterstones specifically.

---

### 3. How do you prioritise requirements

**Answer given:** Requirements are prioritised by product owners at portfolio level. If there is no PO, used a weighted metric — strategic alignment, urgency, impact to revenue, technical feasibility.

**Assessment:** The weighted metric framework is solid and the four criteria are well-chosen. However, given what Martin then described (business functions give requirements directly, no PO), this answer was the most directly relevant question in the interview and needed a concrete anchor. The Costa intake process redesign is the exact story — informal one-liners added to the backlog by arrival date, redesigned to require a structured business case with ROI, sponsorship and strategic alignment, adopted across other business units. That story answers this question with a real outcome and is directly analogous to the Waterstones operating model. It was not used.

---

### 4. What is your favourite ecommerce website

**Answer given:** Amazon — order history, availability of order tracking.

**Assessment:** Safe but flat. Martin came from Amazon — he will have heard this constantly. Order tracking is a functional feature, not a delivery or customer insight. The stronger version picks a site that shows genuine engagement with ecommerce craft and connects it to a specific mechanic or customer observation. For a book retailer interview in particular, connecting to the in-store vs digital discovery gap — curation vs algorithm, serendipity vs search — would have shown exactly the kind of delivery-framed thinking Martin is looking for.

**Stronger version:** Pick a site with a specific feature that demonstrates delivery or UX insight. Structure: name the site, name the feature, connect it to a customer or delivery observation. Example: ASOS size recommendation tool, or a niche retailer with strong editorial curation that mirrors what Waterstones does in-store.

---

### 5. Are you a reader

**Answer given:** Not a reader, but hope that doesn't impact enthusiasm for the role. At Costa, did not like Costa coffee — choice was Nero.

**Assessment:** The Costa and Nero parallel is the best answer in the interview. Authentic, self-aware, memorable and directly analogous. Martin will have heard many forced "I love books" answers — this stands out. The only weakness is that it ended on the parallel without pivoting to what Arpita does connect with about Waterstones — the brand ambition, the in-store and digital challenge, the growth story. The pivot would have turned a great honest answer into a great honest answer that closes on enthusiasm.

### 6. Have you worked with FE tools, JavaScript or database like SQL Server

**Answer given:** Yes, understand JavaScript. Argos catalogues used to be in SQL Server, catalogues changed seasonally. UX teams use Figma, lo-fi wireframes made in Lucidchart.

**Assessment:** The Argos and SQL Server anchor is specific and credible. The Lucidchart wireframing detail is a good signal of proximity to the work. However "I understand JavaScript" is too vague — it does not tell Martin whether she can read code, review a PR, understand a framework or challenge a scope estimate. The answer also jumped across three different tools without structure.

**Stronger version per tool:**
- JS/frontend: "I have not written production JavaScript but I have worked closely enough with frontend engineers to review component architecture and challenge scope estimates — at Estee Lauder I was embedded with the frontend squads end-to-end through the shared component library build"
- SQL: "I have used SQL for data interrogation and validation rather than development — at Argos the product catalogue was in SQL Server and changes were seasonal, so I needed to understand the data model to validate delivery"
- Figma/Lucidchart: "UX teams use Figma; I use Lucidchart for lo-fi wireframing when working through requirements with stakeholders before anything goes to design"

### 8. Tell me about a time you worked with development teams

**Answer given:** Shell — two Agile squads, 15 members each, 4-5 QA, 1 shared Solution Architect. When probed for frontend specifically, mentioned Estee Lauder CX work and the contactless ordering app feature.

**Assessment:** The Shell squad structure is credible but Martin probed for frontend specifically, which means the initial answer did not satisfy. **Stronger version:** Lead with Estee Lauder — embedded with frontend squads, shared component library, checkout flow module and shade selector, specific feature names and business outcome (3% checkout conversion improvement across 17 brands). Use Shell as secondary evidence of Agile squad management at scale.

### 9. Where have you used GenAI

**Answer given:** Using GenAI at ecocentre, run by volunteers. Take product requirement sessions with the Product Owner, then use Copilot to convert meeting notes into user stories with acceptance criteria.

**Assessment:** The strongest answer in the interview. Specific, practical, current usage directly relevant to the TPM role at Waterstones. It also resolves the gap period question (August 2025 onwards) without being asked. The one addition that would have made it stronger: a measurable impact — "this reduced the time from requirements session to backlog-ready stories significantly, and the ACs are more consistent because the format is standardised."

## Role Shape Gap — Key Learning

The prep was built for a programme governance TPM — Shell Steering Committees, multi-vendor management, formal reporting cadences. Martin described an embedded BA-TPM role with a small in-house team. The two models need different prep.

The most relevant story in the entire prep document — Costa Coffee intake process redesign (STAR 7) — was not used. It is a direct analogue for the Waterstones operating model: business functions generating informal requests, no structured prioritisation, TPM redesigning the intake to force clarity and value-based prioritisation. That story answers Q3 (requirements prioritisation) with a real outcome and matches the role exactly.

**Before the next interview:** Ask the recruiter how the TPM interfaces with developers day-to-day and whether there is a Product Owner. The answer determines whether to lead with Shell or Costa stories.

## Patterns to Fix Before Next Interview

1. **Tell me about yourself must be delivered as prepped** — the opening answer was improvised rather than delivered as written. The numbers (10M+ customers, four markets, tripled velocity) are what validate seniority. Without them the intro is generic.
2. **Match the STAR to the role shape** — programme governance stories (Shell Steering Committee) were prepped for a role that needed embedded team stories (Costa intake redesign). Confirm role shape before building prep.
3. **Frontend story must lead, not recover** — when working with dev teams is asked at a small in-house team, the Estee Lauder frontend story is the primary answer. Shell squad management is the secondary. Not the other way around.
4. **Favourite website needs delivery insight, not a feature list** — picking Amazon and citing order tracking is safe but does not show delivery or customer thinking. Pick a site with a specific mechanic and connect it to an observation.
5. **Culture fit pivots need a close** — the Costa and Nero answer was strong but ended on the parallel. Always pivot to what you do connect with about the brand's challenge or ambition.
6. **GenAI answer was the strongest** — ecocentre requirements session to user stories with ACs is specific, current and directly relevant. Add a measurable impact line for future interviews.

Dear Nomaan, Sagheer and the Safeguarding officer at Bushey CC,


Thank you for giving me the opportunity to respond to the concerns raised by West Herts following our 3rd XI fixture against West Herts
5th XI on Saturday 27 June 2026.


I take any safeguarding matter extremely seriously, particularly where junior players are involved. I would therefore like to set out my
account clearly, fairly and in context. I have also asked Hamza Wahid to provide a comprehensive statement in response to the allegation
made against him, and I have attached that statement herewith.


I strongly deny that I threatened, or instructed any of my adult players to target, West Herts junior players. The specific phrase attributed to
me by Dillon Mahawatte — “Just wait. When your youngsters come out to bat, I’m going to get my adults to have a go at them” — is
something I never said.


What I did say was made in the context of West Herts juniors sledging our senior batters during our innings, which took place first. A
number of their juniors were very vocal, including their wicket keeper, Sachin Kanuga, and their fielder at gully, Kumaran Thangiah, who
made comments towards our senior batter, Waseem Saber.


As I was at the non-striker’s end at the time, I called over the West Herts captain, Dillon Mahawatte, and asked him to speak to his juniors
and discourage that behaviour. Dillon laughed and responded along the lines of, “Are you going to complain about a 14-year-old making
noise?” While I was speaking to Dillon, the fielder at midwicket, Prad Kanuga, who I later learned is Sachin Kanuga’s father, became
involved in the conversation. I felt he spoke to me in a disrespectful manner and defended the juniors’ behaviour rather than helping to
calm the situation.


Realising that the West Herts senior players did not appear willing to discourage this behaviour, I made a light-hearted but advisory
comment to their captain that if it continued, we would have to do the same when their juniors came out to bat. This was not intended as a
threat. It was a warning about the impact of allowing sledging to continue unchecked and was said in an attempt to make the point that
such behaviour should not be encouraged from either side. I also want to make clear that I continued to have cordial conversations with
both Sachin and Kumaran afterwards during our batting innings.


I am concerned that this comment has now been presented as, “I’m going to get my adults to have a go at them.” That is not what I said,
and I believe the way this has been reported materially changes the meaning and tone of the exchange. In my view, this raises concerns
about the accuracy and fairness of the accounts being provided by West Herts. I never made such a comment to any junior player. The
whole purpose of my comment to the captain was to de-escalate the situation at an early stage so that matters did not get out of hand.


I also wish to give wider context about the conduct of some West Herts senior players, particularly Prad Kanuga, during the match. During
our batting innings, while our junior batter, Shay Solanki, was batting, Prad continued to engage with him and appeared to ridicule aspects
of his batting technique. When Shay was eventually dismissed, their bowler, Champaka Ramanayake, a former Sri Lankan Test cricketer,
gave Shay a visual send-off. Prad then continued with the same sort of behaviour even after the 14-year-old had been dismissed.


When Shay came back to the changing area, he said words to the effect of, “He’s picking on a 14-year-old,” referring to Prad. Shay was
visibly upset, and I understand that his mother was also upset after learning what had happened. Later, during West Herts’ batting innings,
Shay was bowling and Prad again made comments towards him, which also appeared to upset him. This added to my concern that the
behaviour towards Shay was not limited to one isolated incident.


During West Herts’ batting innings, Prad Kanuga acted as their main umpire. On one occasion, one of our bowlers, Hamza Wahid, handed
his cap, sunglasses and mobile phone to the umpire, as is common while bowling. Prad initially refused to take the sunglasses and mobile

behaviour.

line clearly. Prad was ahead of me and, in my view, was in an even less favourable position to make a certain call that the ball had crossed

position.


It was therefore a case of whether to accept the account of an honest fielder who was closest to the incident, or to accept the umpire’s
decision when, from my position, it appeared clear that he could not have seen whether the ball had crossed the line. That is why I
